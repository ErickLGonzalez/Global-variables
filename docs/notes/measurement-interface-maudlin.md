**Paper:** Tim Maudlin, "Actual Physics, Observation, and Quantum Theory," arXiv:2512.22618, physics.hist-ph, Dec 27 2025 (forthcoming in *How to Understand Quantum Mechanics?*, Springer). Ledger entry R14.

**Classification: METHOD.** No theorems, no data, no matrix-cell changes. Its value is a sustained, historically grounded analysis of exactly the layer our decoding pipeline calls step 1: separating 𝒰 (the unknown rule system) from 𝓜 (the measurement interface) in y = 𝓜[𝒰(x)] + ε.

## The argument, compressed

1. Einstein against Heisenberg: theories are not built from observable quantities; "it is the theory which decides what we can observe." Einstein's schema A → S → E: axioms, derived statements, experiential data — with the S↔E bridge *extralogical*.
2. The E's must be couched in macroscopic local-beable language (positions/motions of middle-sized objects), not subjective experience — Bohr's "classical concepts" read charitably, Bell's local beables as the programmatic requirement on theory ontology.
3. The "measurement problem as it appears in Relativity": Einstein himself flagged that clocks and rods are *stipulated*, not derived from the field equations — a self-conscious inconsistency incurred as an obligation to be discharged later. All actual empirical contact works this way; the Laplace/Bošković demon method is never what real science does.
4. Applied to QM: the standard recipe abstracts the apparatus into a Hermitian operator/POVM with no principled device→operator association; even the two-slit experiment's contact with data runs through un-derived stipulations (the screen).
5. Payoff claim: taking the interface seriously points at experiments — notably **arrival times** — where the standard formalism underdetermines the prediction and rival completions (e.g., Bohmian trajectory theories) differ measurably.
